Tips & starting out

Honest advice for getting going — and for when you're already going but confused.

Starting out

The single best thing you can do as a beginner is pick a project that is smaller than you think is interesting. A tote bag before a dress. A pillowcase before a shirt. Not because you can't do more — but because finishing something teaches you more than half-finishing something ambitious. Confidence is the first skill.

On machines: you do not need to buy a new one to start. A decent secondhand mechanical machine — a Janome, a Singer, a Brother from the 1990s or 2000s — will sew circles around a cheap new one. Look on Marktplaats, ask in the chat, or check with local sewing schools who often sell their old machines. Budget €80–150 for something reliable. Get it serviced if it hasn't been used in years.

On patterns: start with something that has the word "easy", "beginner", or "< 2 hours" in the description. Patterns with fewer than 8 pattern pieces. Elastic waistbands rather than zips. Knit fabric is actually forgiving of wonky seams — it stretches. Woven cotton doesn't lie. Both are fine to start with.

Pressing matters more than most tutorials admit. Iron every seam after you sew it — before you sew the next piece. A £15 secondhand iron does this perfectly well. A pressing cloth (a piece of cotton muslin works) saves your fabrics from shine marks.

Reading a Dutch fabric label

When you're at a Dutch fabric shop, the bolt ends and labels are often only in Dutch. Here's what the most common bits mean:

100% katoen  /  breedte 140cm  /  krimpvrij
  • 100% katoen — 100% cotton. The fibre content. Always check this.
  • breedte 140cm — width is 140cm. This affects how much you buy. A 90cm-wide fabric requires more metres for the same pattern.
  • krimpvrij — shrink-resistant or pre-shrunk. Good, but still worth a pre-wash on anything you're putting skin-close or that will be washed regularly.

Other common label words: wasbaar (washable), droogbaar (tumble-dry safe), strijken (ironing), op kleur wassen (wash with similar colours), niet bleken (do not bleach). The care symbols are the same international ones — the tub, the triangle, the iron, the circle.

Where to learn more

The internet has an enormous amount of sewing content — here are some starting points that have earned genuine community trust:

  • Pattern Review — user reviews and ratings of thousands of patterns. Check before you buy.
  • Threads Magazine — deep technique articles, fitting advice, and project guides. US-focused but universally useful.
  • Grainline Studio blog — well-written posts on fit and technique from indie pattern designer Jen Beeman.
  • Chelsea Sews (YouTube) — beginner-friendly tutorials with good camera work and clear explanations.
  • r/sewing — community questions, project sharing, and advice. Use the search before posting — most beginner questions have been answered well.

FAQ

How much fabric do I actually need to buy?

Your pattern will tell you — but always add at least 10% for pre-washing shrinkage, cutting mistakes, and the grain-line panic that hits at 11pm. Patterns usually give yardage for 60" (150cm) and sometimes 45" (115cm) width. If your fabric is narrower than the pattern assumes, you'll need more.

As a rough guide: a basic top takes 1–1.5m; a dress 2–3m; trousers 1.5–2.5m; a full coat 3–4m+. These are not precise — always follow the pattern.

Do I need to pre-wash fabric before cutting?

For natural fibres (cotton, linen, viscose) — yes, almost always. They can shrink 5–10% in the first wash. Pre-wash at the temperature you plan to wash the finished garment.

For polyester and other synthetics — usually not essential, but still worth a gentle wash to remove any manufacturing coatings that can affect how the fabric handles.

For fabric you plan to dry-clean — follow the cleaning instructions on the bolt.

What's the difference between a serger / overlocker and a regular sewing machine?

A regular sewing machine sews a lockstitch — two threads interlocking. It's what you use for the main construction seams, topstitching, buttonholes, and most sewing tasks.

A serger (called an overlocker in Dutch: overlocker or lockmachine) uses 3–5 threads to wrap the edge of the fabric, trim the seam allowance, and finish in one pass. It gives that professional ready-to-wear edge inside garments. You don't need one to start — many excellent sewists use pinking shears or a zigzag stitch on their regular machine for years.

I'm buying fabric in a Dutch shop — how do I ask for a half metre?

"Mag ik een halve meter, alsjeblieft?" — May I have half a metre, please?

"Twee meter, alsjeblieft." — Two metres, please.

Most shop staff in Amsterdam are comfortable with English too, but making the attempt in Dutch is always appreciated.

My seams are puckering. What's wrong?

Thread tension is the usual culprit — try adjusting the upper tension dial by small increments. Wrong needle for the fabric is another common cause: a ballpoint needle for knits, a sharp for wovens, a microtex for silk. Cheap thread tangles and breaks at the needle; switching to a better thread (Gutermann, Gütermann, Coats) often fixes persistent puckering.

Also: sew with the fabric relaxed on the machine — don't pull it through. Let the feed dogs do their job.

Can I join the chat if I'm a complete beginner?

Absolutely — the community is for everyone who sews or wants to start. There are no minimum skill requirements. The chat language is a mix of Dutch, English, and Russian, and everyone's welcome to write in whatever language they're most comfortable with.

What is a toile / muslin and do I really need to make one?

A toile (in Dutch: proefexemplaar; in the US called a muslin) is a test version of your garment sewn in cheap fabric before cutting into your good fabric. It lets you check the fit and spot problems before they're expensive.

For beginner projects — simple tops, elastic-waist skirts, tote bags — no, you probably don't need one. For anything with a fitted bodice, structured jacket, or expensive fabric — yes, really, make the toile. The hour it takes will save three hours of frustrated unpicking.

Where can I find a fabric market in the Netherlands?

The Lapjesmarkt in Utrecht happens on Saturday mornings and is one of the best general fabric markets in the country. Amsterdam's Albert Cuypmarkt has a handful of fabric stalls. Various cities also have occasional fabric-specific markets — keep an eye on the community chat for announcements, or search "lapjesmarkt" with your city name.
